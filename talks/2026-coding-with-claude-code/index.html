Coding with Claude Code

The disciplined engineering loop for computational-materials research: seven phases, the constraint theory underneath them, and one units bug that only a human caught.

Foundations · why the workflow has this shape · ~9 min

The constraints that force the procedure

  • Smart zone / dumb zone: effective context is far shorter than the advertised window
  • A bigger window doesn't move that limit, it just ships more dumb zone
  • Feedback loops are the ceiling, not the model, not the prompt
  • Tokens are a metered second compute budget, and mirror the KMC engine
  • A procedure you don't understand is one you'll abandon when rushed
The seven phases are the what. This section is the why, the handful of facts about LLMs, feedback, and code that force the workflow into its shape. I'm doing this because a procedure you don't understand is a procedure you'll abandon the first time it's inconvenient: you'll skip the grilling when you're in a hurry, let the agent QA its own physics because reading numbers is boring, keep one giant context window open all day because clearing feels wasteful. Four constraints. First: the model has a smart zone and a dumb zone, and the boundary (a model-specific rule of thumb, often quoted near 100K tokens, which I qualify on the next slides) is not the advertised context limit. A 200K or a million-token window doesn't move that effective limit; it just ships you more dumb zone. Second, and this is the load-bearing claim of the whole workshop: the quality of your feedback loops is the ceiling on how well an AI can work in your code. Not the model. Not the prompt. The feedback. Third: tokens are a metered second compute budget on top of your wall-clock, and, beautifully, the token machinery is structurally isomorphic to the pyKMC engine itself. Let me take each in a drill-down.

Smart zone / dumb zone: a correctness control

  • Cost and quality are two facts, not one: the quadratic doesn't cause the degradation
  • Cost: attention is O(n²) in length (compute and memory), so big contexts are expensive
  • Quality: positional bias degrades use well before the window fills (Lost in the Middle, TACL 2024; RULER)
  • '~100K' is a model-specific heuristic, not a measured constant
  • For us the dumb zone manufactures silently-wrong physics: size to the smart zone, clear don't compact

pykmc/htst/constants.py: omega_eV_to_hz() and hz_to_per_ps() are two functions because they are two facts, the fine-grained correctness the agent holds in the smart zone and drops in the dumb zone.

Why is there a dumb zone? Two separate facts, which this talk used to merge and shouldn't. The first is cost: self-attention is order n-squared in sequence length, because every token attends to every other token, so doubling the context roughly quadruples the attention compute and memory. That is a statement about cost, not about quality; resist the temptation to say the quadratic is what makes the model dumber, because an ML-literate audience will catch it. The second, separate fact is quality: long contexts are used unevenly. Liu and colleagues, 'Lost in the Middle' (TACL 2024), show a U-shaped curve, performance highest when the relevant information sits at the start or end of the context and significantly worse when it is buried in the middle; effective-context benchmarks like RULER confirm the usable length is far shorter than the advertised window. The '~100K' figure is a practitioner heuristic, model-specific, not a measured constant, so I present it as a labelled rule of thumb, not a published threshold. Wherever the boundary sits for a given model, a web app degrading there forgets a prop and ships a visible glitch; a simulation degrading there produces a trajectory full of plausible hops with a barrier quietly off by a factor you won't notice until someone reruns it. The dumb zone, for us, is where silently-wrong physics is manufactured. So sizing a task to fit the smart zone is not productivity advice; it's a correctness control. Concretely: in my constants file there are two functions, omega_eV_to_hz and hz_to_per_ps, kept separate because they are two different facts, exactly the fine-grained distinction a sharp agent holds and a saturated one drops. And the corollary: clear, don't compact. A cleared context is the Memento reset, amnesia, but the same amnesia every time, which is reproducible. A compacted context carries forward the model's own summary of its own wrong turn, sediment you can't inspect. Durable knowledge therefore lives in contracts and docs, not in conversation.

The ceiling claim, unpacked. A web app's feedback loops are types, unit tests, lint, a screenshot. We have all of those plus a second tier the software world doesn't have: the physics itself, as automatable checks that fail loudly. Detailed balance must hold, a barrier must match an independent NEB, MSD must be linear after the transient, a first-order saddle must have exactly one imaginary mode, a prefactor must be roughly terahertz, not hertz, not petahertz. And here is the most dangerous failure mode in our field: green and wrong. In the units bug, several pre-existing tests asserted rates built from the raw Hz value, so the suite stayed green while every HTST rate was ten-to-the-twelfth too large. The tests had encoded the error and were faithfully defending it. So a human reading the numbers is part of the loop, not a courtesy after it. Two consequences for code shape: deep modules, small interface over large implementation, give you one clean seam to wrap a test around, so deep modules lead to testability lead to strong feedback loops lead to good agent work; and the contrapositive, a bad codebase makes a bad agent, where the 'in' of garbage-in-garbage-out is your architecture. One honest tension to name out loud, since I lean on Ousterhout for deep modules: Ousterhout is publicly skeptical of strict test-driven development, arguing it fixates on getting specific features working rather than finding the best design. So I'm not claiming TDD drives good design here. The regression test earns its keep on a different and more defensible basis: it pins a physical contract, a correctness guarantee that must stay red until the units and the barrier are right, independent of how the solver is structured. My A/B harness is a physics-tier loop: paired constant-versus-htst runs on matched seeds, built to fail if HTST produces no finite nu0. Building loops like that raises the ceiling instead of nudging the agent under a fixed one.

Tokens. Two payloads. First a cost model: tokens are a second metered budget, billed per call, input priced separately from output. 'hello world' cost 11 tokens on one provider, 4 on another, for identical meaning; cost tracks encoding, not content. The discipline that follows: feed the agent a summary contract plus a file path, never the dump. In one validation run my trajectory is over a megabyte, the reference table is 5.7 megabytes, a single LAMMPS log is 11, pasting any of those pays, by the token, to re-encode a structure the engine already holds on disk. Second payload, and the more important one: pyKMC already does, on atomic environments, exactly what a tokenizer does on text. reference_table.pickle IS a learned vocabulary; the KMC main loop IS an encode-compute-decode pipeline. The per-step bookkeeping is literally a hit/miss counter: n_current is a vocabulary hit, a free lookup; n_new is a miss that triggers twenty ARTn saddle searches, the expensive case, just like an unfamiliar word splitting into four tokens. The Ref Event index is a token id, not the physics. And here's the punchline that connects to the bug: decode is the step that 'just joins the numbers back,' and it silently corrupts when the number you join carries a different unit than the consumer assumes. The Hz-to-per-picosecond bug is, precisely, a decode failure: the engine thought fluently in the wrong currency.

The Seven Phases · the spine of the method · ~11 min

Idea → Research → Prototype → PRD → Kanban → Execution → QA

  • Two conditional phases: Research (2) and Prototype (3), skipped when small/familiar
  • Last three (Kanban→Execution→QA) form a loop until the science converges
  • QA failures become new tickets → back to Kanban
  • A map, not a gate: tiny work skips 2, 3, most of 4
  • Value: names for the steps, so nobody silently drops QA
Now the spine. Serious AI-assisted work, whether a web app or a one-line fix, moves through seven phases, and naming them keeps you and the agent honest. In order: Idea, Research, Prototype, PRD, Kanban, Execution, QA. Two of them are conditional, Research and Prototype, and you skip them when the work is small or familiar. The last three, Kanban, Execution, QA, form a loop you traverse until the science converges; QA failures become new tickets and send you back to Kanban. The thing to hold onto: this is a map, not a gate. For a one-flag tweak you skip straight to a tiny execution and a QA glance. The payoff of having the map is that neither you nor the agent ever silently drops QA, and QA, for us, is where the science is made or lost. Phases two through four are where you stop fooling yourself before you spend compute. Let me walk the conditional front half and the PRD, then I'll give Execution and QA their own sections because that's where research diverges hardest from product work.

In the video, each PRD item carries a boolean passes flag, and the agent sets it itself: implements the feature, runs type-check and tests, sees green, flips passes to true. For a web app that's defensible, the tests are the behaviour the user sees. For physics it is a trap, for the reason we already met: a green suite tells you the code ran, it never tells you the physics is right, and our worst bug lived comfortably inside a green suite. So in a research Ralph the passes predicate is not 'tests pass.' It is a machine-checkable acceptance band on a physical quantity, in electron-volts, angstroms, picoseconds, evaluated by running the real solver. Two concrete examples from my code. First, require_one_negative_mode: it reads the actual dynamical matrix and demands exactly one imaginary mode at the saddle; the agent cannot fake that with a passing unit test. Second, the finite-nu0 A/B gate in run_htst_ab.py: its overall status is 'passed' only if a failures list is empty, and that list is built from physics observables: no reference events, no nu0 column, no finite nu0 values. Notice coverage below 1.0 is a logged warning, not a failure, a smoke run that hit only 33% coverage was recorded honestly as partial, not a benchmark, not silently passed off as done. The rule to hand an agent: every passes flag points at a runnable predicate over a physical quantity in physical units. The agent may write and run it; it may not substitute 'the tests are green' for 'the physics is in band.' The other three disciplines, quickly. Memory: a Ralph agent is memory-less, every iteration starts blank and reconstructs what happened from the queue and a progress file, so the instruction must be append, never update; tell it to 'update progress' and it rewrites the whole file and destroys history. And for us the progress note should record the physics state, not just the code state: 'backfilled nu0 for the reference events, coverage 0.71, three events fell back to k0 because one negative mode failed', that's the thing the next blank-slate iteration can't cheaply re-derive. Sizing: exactly one feature per iteration, small and roughly equal, because the model gets dumber as its window fills and a small task leaves budget to actually verify the change. This is literal, not metaphor: computing a Vineyard prefactor means diagonalizing a Hessian at each event; doing it as one monolithic 'compute all prefactors' step is the oversized item, so commit 82e7793 shards it into bounded per-event batches, with the batch size chosen empirically by that earlier profiling harness. Commits: one per iteration makes the history bisectable, which is exactly why, when the units bug surfaced, I could bisect straight to the boundary where nu0 entered the rate path and land the fix as its own one-line commit in its own worktree. And the role shift, which is the human payoff: the old multi-phase-plan workflow made you an anal-retentive planner routing every task by hand. Ralph demotes you into the requirements-gatherer seat: you stop specifying how the work is done and specify what must be true at the end, then read whether the numbers landed in band. The science is made in what you choose to require.

Context & Handoff · surviving many fresh agents · ~4 min

Compaction buys continuity; handoff buys purity

  • Grep the convergence line, not the log: 30 tokens vs 30,000
  • Compaction is lossy by omission you don't control: drops the load-bearing caveat
  • Handoff: a disposable temp-dir pointer doc carrying one task's slice
  • Point, not copy: references stay correct as targets evolve
  • Portable markdown → adversarial physics review by a different agent
Briefly, the primitive that lets a multi-phase campaign survive a finite, degrading window. First habit: grep the convergence line, not the log, a grep returning three lines costs about 30 tokens; reading the file costs 30,000. So watch lammps.log and artn.out, don't read them; pull the header of a structure file, not the coordinate block. When you approach the dumb zone you have two moves. Compaction summarizes the conversation and continues, fine for one long single-threaded debug, but lossy by omission you don't control: it keeps what looks salient and silently drops what looks like a footnote, and in our world the footnote is the load-bearing physics, 'nu0 here is in Hz, not per-picosecond.' Treat a compaction summary the way you treat a green suite: it is not validated context. The alternative is a handoff: a small disposable markdown doc, written to a temp directory, carrying only the slice a fresh agent needs for one task. Compaction buys continuity; handoff buys purity, and for a campaign that fans out into eighteen worktrees, purity is the property that scales. Two rules: point, don't copy, reference the issue, the committed input, the RESULTS file, so the pointer stays correct as the target evolves; and because it's plain markdown it crosses agent boundaries, which buys you the highest-value research use, hand the same doc to a different agent and tell it to disbelieve the numbers. An independent agent that doesn't share your assumptions is the closest automated analogue to the human who finally catches that THz, per-picosecond, and Hz are not interchangeable.

A post-checkout git hook re-runs the wiring script on every git worktree add, so a brand-new worktree populates itself with no extra commands. And the decision rule from the spine slide is what decides what goes where: a durable fact becomes a rule in AGENTS.md, a repeatable procedure becomes a skill, an occasional heavy pipeline becomes a dispatched agent, and a hard invariant becomes a hook. You do not need to copy my repository; you need the split and a one-line wiring script.

drill-down · trigger surface

When NOT to make a skill: the trigger-surface cost

  • Every always-on skill description is read every turn: it competes for smart-zone attention
  • A description is a trigger ('use when ...'), not a summary ('this does ...')
  • Too many skills, or over-broad ones, and the model misfires or drowns
  • Prefer a one-paragraph rule, or a dispatched agent, over a new always-on skill
  • superpowers' '1% chance, you MUST invoke' is exactly the over-trigger to govern

The dumb-zone argument applies to your own tooling: a skill you rarely use still spends attention every turn it is not used. Off the trigger surface (a dispatched agent) costs nothing until called.

This is the deeper why behind the taxonomy slide, and it connects straight back to the foundations. Every skill you install carries an always-on description, and the model reads all of those descriptions every single turn to decide what might apply. That is the trigger surface, and it is not free: it is exactly the smart-zone attention the constraint-theory section said is scarce, so a wall of skills is self-inflicted dumb zone. Two consequences. First, the description is a trigger, not a summary: write use this when stress-testing a plan, not this skill interviews the user, because the model matches on when. Second, and this is the when-not-to rule, most candidate skills should not be skills: if it is a durable fact, make it a one-paragraph rule in the contract, which is read once at session start, not re-evaluated every turn; if it is an occasional heavy pipeline, make it a dispatched agent, which sits off the trigger surface and costs nothing until you call it. The worked example is the one I named earlier: the superpowers pack ships an instruction that a one-percent chance a skill might apply means you must invoke it, which is the textbook over-trigger, and governing it down is how you keep the surface honest. The rule of thumb: a skill you rarely use still taxes you every turn it sits unused, so the bar for an always-on skill is high.

drill-down · HPC

Running it for real: SLURM on the Alliance (DRAC)

  • Submit and watch: sbatch, salloc, squeue/sq, sacct, scancel
  • Always required: --time and --account (plus --mem / --mem-per-cpu, --gpus-per-node)
  • $SLURM_TMPDIR is fast node-local scratch, deleted at job end; module load for the stack
  • /home (small, backed up) vs /scratch (big, purged) vs /project (shared); Globus for big transfers; one array job, not 1000 sbatch calls
  • Security: never an unattended Ralph with --dangerously-skip-permissions on a login node or with live credentials; the sandbox is the boundary

A study run = an array job over seeds/temps, each staging inputs into $SLURM_TMPDIR, launching pyKMC under srun, copying results back to /project before the node is wiped. The Ralph loop runs inside a container/VM, never with live cluster credentials.

This audience runs on the Digital Research Alliance of Canada, so the workflow has to land on SLURM. The verbs: sbatch to submit a batch script, salloc for an interactive allocation, squeue or the Alliance shortcut sq to watch the queue, sacct for accounting on finished jobs, scancel to kill one. Two flags are non-negotiable on the Alliance: --time, because every job needs a wall-clock estimate, and --account, because compute is billed against an allocation; then --mem or --mem-per-cpu, and --gpus-per-node if you need a GPU. The filesystem distinction bites people: /home is small and backed up, /scratch is large but purged on a schedule, /project is shared and quota'd; and $SLURM_TMPDIR is node-local scratch that is fast and is deleted the instant the job ends, so you stage in, compute, and copy results out before exit. Use module load to bring up the toolchain, Globus for large transfers, and array jobs for parameter sweeps rather than a shell loop firing thousands of sbatch calls, which annoys the scheduler and your neighbours. The security line I will not soften: never run an unattended Ralph loop with --dangerously-skip-permissions on a login node, or anywhere it can touch live cluster or credential access. The security boundary is the sandbox, a container, a devcontainer, a fresh worktree, or a VM, not the agent's good behaviour.

drill-down · reproducibility

The artifact is reproducible even though the agent is not

  • Agent runs are non-deterministic; AI projects often don't even execute (~31.7% fail out of the box)
  • The reproducible unit is NOT the agent transcript
  • It is: committed code + pinned env (container/lockfile) + fixed RNG seed + the regression test
  • Declared vs runtime dependencies drifted ~13.5x in that study: pin them
  • Same discipline as any KMC study: fix the seed, archive the inputs, pin the physical contract

Reproducible study = fixed RNG seed for the BKL draws + pinned EAM potential + archived reference_table.pickle + the Ni(100) dE regression test. Rerunnable next year regardless of which agent wrote it. (arXiv:2512.22387)

The single biggest gap a careful audience will name: how can a non-deterministic agent produce reproducible science? It can't, and you should say so plainly. An empirical study of Claude Code, Codex and Gemini across 300 projects (arXiv 2512.22387) found only about 68 percent executed out of the box; the remaining roughly 31.7 percent failed immediately, and declared-versus-runtime dependencies expanded by something like 13.5 times. So the reproducible unit is not the agent transcript, which you will never replay. It is the committed code, a pinned environment (a container image or a lockfile), a fixed RNG seed for the stochastic draws, and the regression test that pins the physical contract. That is exactly the discipline we already apply to a KMC campaign: fix the seed for the BKL clock, archive the EAM potential and the inputs, and keep the Ni(100) barrier regression test. Make 'the artifact is reproducible even though the agent is not' an explicit claim, because it converts a worry into a checklist.

drill-down · failure modes

The units bug is one genre: a taxonomy

  • Slopsquatting: ~19.7% of recommended packages don't exist (USENIX Security 2025); a fabricated import is a supply-chain hole
  • Insecure code: ~45% of samples carried an OWASP Top-10 vulnerability (Veracode 2025)
  • Higher defect density: AI pull requests ~1.7x the issues of human ones (CodeRabbit 2025)
  • Domain silent killers: wrong constants, off-by-one lattice indexing, green-but-wrong tests
  • The units bug is the last row; no linter catches it, only a human reading the physics

The Hz to ps⁻¹ bug is the 'green-but-wrong test' genre: it ran, the suite was green, the numbers were 10¹² off. The taxonomy says the units bug was not bad luck, it was a predictable category.

My talk has one gorgeous bug; the audience needs the genre map so they don't think it was a freak. Four families. First, hallucinated packages, nicknamed slopsquatting: Spracklen and colleagues (USENIX Security 2025) found across 576,000 samples that roughly 19.7 percent of recommended packages did not exist, worse for open models; a fabricated import is a live supply-chain attack surface, because an attacker can register the hallucinated name. Second, insecure code: the Veracode 2025 GenAI report, across 100-plus models and 80 tasks, found about 45 percent of cases produced code with an OWASP Top-10 vulnerability, Java worst. Third, higher defect density: CodeRabbit's 2025 analysis of 470 pull requests found AI PRs averaged about 1.7 times the issues of human PRs. Fourth, and most relevant to us, the domain-specific silent killers: subtly wrong algorithms, incorrect physical constants, off-by-one in lattice indexing, and green-but-wrong tests. My Hz-to-per-picosecond bug is squarely in that last family. The lesson is that the units bug was not bad luck; it was a predictable category, and the defense for that category is a human who reads the physics, because no linter, type checker, or unit test sees it.

  • "How is a stochastic run reproducible?" It isn't; the pinned artifact is
  • "Does this fit open science / FAIR?" Yes: version prompts and CLAUDE.md, disclose AI use, share the artifact

The four objections I always get, with the answers I actually give. Deskilling: real risk, and the framing that holds is division of labour, AI as the tactical programmer that knows syntax and common patterns, the human as the strategic designer who owns system boundaries, the physics, and the definition of done; the antidote to erosion is keeping judgment human and reviewing every diff, not abstaining. Phase 7: the units bug, and the one thing to remember

  • nu0 emitted in Hz (~1–10 THz), consumed as ps⁻¹ → every rate 10¹² too large
  • Nothing crashed; the suite was green; the tests had pinned the error
  • Fix d705932: one-line hz_to_per_ps() at the backend boundary
  • Regression d6b921f: red by 10¹² before, green after, at the Ni(100) dE where both threads meet
  • QA = validating the physics. Green tests are not validated physics.
Phase 7, QA, and the landing. The units bug is the whole talk in one line of code. The Vineyard formula emits nu0 as a linear frequency in hertz, roughly 1 to 10 terahertz for FCC nickel, but pyKMC's rate layer and its KMC clock both consume the prefactor in per-picosecond, and the backends passed nu0 straight through, unconverted. Because the prefactor multiplies directly into k equals nu0 times the Boltzmann factor, treating one hertz as one per-picosecond made every HTST rate ten-to-the-twelfth too large and every residence time ten-to-the-twelfth too small. Nothing crashed. The simulation ran, produced numbers, and the numbers were silently, totally wrong, and worse, several pre-existing tests had asserted rates built from the raw Hz value, so the suite was green while the physics was wrong; the tests had encoded and pinned the error. No test, type-checker, or linter could have caught this. Only a human who understood the physics did. The fix was one line: convert from Hz to per-picosecond at the backend boundary. And the regression test, commit d6b921f, drives a known nu0 at the committed Ni(100) surface-hop dE, exactly where my two threads meet, the slabs measuring that barrier and the rate code consuming it, through the real backend and the real clock: red by ten-to-the-twelfth before the fix, green after. So the one thing to take home: QA, for us, is validating the physics, and green tests are not validated physics. The defense is a human who treats THz, per-picosecond, and Hz as not interchangeable, and the entire workflow exists to put that human, in the smart zone, on the most dangerous question: are these numbers right?
